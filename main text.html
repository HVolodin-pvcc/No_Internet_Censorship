Increases in Internet Censorship

Recently, internet censorship has been on the rise.
Governments have been restricting what their citizens can say, especially when it comes to online speech.
This is bad, because the internet requires a free distribution of ideas in order to be what it is today,
that is being forum of information, both good and bad. Internet censorship has been happening in 3 areas of note,
that being the United States of America, European countries like Germany, and China

In order to maintain or expand our rights, we need to do multiple things.
For one, when the government infringes on your rights as a citizen, you need to protest so that you can
maintain your rights, instead of letting them be stepped on. In addition, it helps to
read up on legislation that is being debated, so that you can be aware of what is happening
in the government. Finally, you need to resist apathy. When a citizen decides that their rights are not
worth fighting for, that is when censorship wins, as you cannot protect rights that people do not want.

America Germany China
While this may come as a surprise to people living in America, Internet freedom has decreased
in the United States over the past five years. During the 2020 Election,
government authorities were found to have responded to protests with surveillance
and online harassment, as noted by Freedom House. Freedom House continued by
explaining that American copyright law allows companies to censor opinions.
“Section 512 of the DMCA, enacted in 1998, created new immunity
from copyright claims for online service providers.
However, the law’s notice-and-takedown requirements
have been criticized for impinging on speech rights,
as they may incentivize platforms to remove potentially
unlawful content without meaningful judicial oversight.
Early research on the DMCA found that notice-and-takedown procedures were
sometimes used “to stifle criticism, commentary, and fair use.”
In other instances, overly broad or fraudulent DMCA claims resulted in the removal of
content that should be excused under provisions for free expression, fair use, or education.
DMCA complaints have also been used as a
vehicle for taking down political campaign advertisements.”		 In Europe, however, internet censorship has a larger prevalence. In Germany, for example,
the police can come and inquire about comments made by relatives, even if there is
not a direct call to violence. The New York Times said “They told him that his 51-year-old father
was accused of violating laws against online hate speech, insults and misinformation. He had
shared an image on Facebook with an inflammatory statement about immigration falsely
attributed to a German politician. 'Just because someone rapes, robs or is a serious criminal is not a reason
for deportation,'' the fake remark said. The police then scoured the home for about 30 minutes, seizing a laptop and tablet
as evidence, prosecutors said. At that exact moment in March, a similar scene was playing out at about
100 other homes across Germany, part of a coordinated nationwide crackdown that continues to this day.
After sharing images circulating on Facebook that carried a fake statement,
the perpetrators had devices confiscated and some were fined. “We are making it clear that anyone
who posts hate messages must expect the police to be at the front door afterward,”
Holger Münch, the head of the Federal Criminal Police Office,
said after the March raids. This is important, because it means that in Germany,
your rights can be infringed and you can be arrested for hate speech,
which can mean many things. Hate speech has unfortunately been
interpreted in many ways, and can mean that you will be charged with
the crime of your own opinions, which you are entitled to have.		 China is where internet censorship is at its worst. The government regulates all
aspects of the internet, preventing any discussion or opposition. Freedom House
rated China at 9/100 in internet freedom, stating
“Conditions for internet users in China remained
profoundly oppressive and confirmed the
country’s status as the world’s worst abuser
of internet freedom for the ninth consecutive year. Unprecedented protests were
mobilized against the government’s COVID-19 policy
during the coverage period. Though authorities responded with
swift censorship, protesters successfully pressured the
government into withdrawing its draconian zero-COVID policy.
Ordinary users continued to face severe legal and extralegal
repercussions for activities like sharing news stories, talking about their
religious beliefs, or communicating with
family members and others overseas.
Separately, authorities wielded immense power over
the technology industry through new
legislation, regulatory investigations, and app-store removals.
Authorities also imposed new restrictions on generative
artificial-intelligence (AI) tools during the coverage period.”
This is censorship at its absolute worst, where no differing ideas
are tolerated or discussed, for fear of repercussions from the government.
